# Visual Excellence Patterns

This reference provides positive thinking patterns for visual decision-making. It complements the diagnostic frameworks (hook science, energy density, visual contrast) with constructive guidance on what excellent execution looks like and how to think toward it.

The goal: when building a scene, the creator should not be checking a list of prohibitions. They should be asking "what is the single best way to serve THIS scene's job?"

## The Hero Frame Principle

Some videos have one frame that carries the thesis's most powerful visual proof or payoff. Use a hero frame when that is true; do not force one onto montage, observational, or rhythm-led work.

Finding it:
- which scene contains the most surprising fact, the strongest evidence, or the most memorable number?
- if a viewer could only see ONE still image from this video, which scene would make them understand and feel the thesis?
- when one candidate clearly carries the film, treat it as the hero frame and give it the most deliberate composition.

How to use it:
- identify an optional hero scene during Lean scene production, not as a mandatory planning ritual
- record it in `extensions.visual_intent.hero_scene_id` only when selected
- during visual direction, design the hero frame FIRST. Then design the scenes around it to create contrast that makes the hero frame stand out more.
- the hero frame's dominant element should be 2-3x larger than anything else in the video
- if no single frame should dominate, invest in sequence contrast, continuity, or rhythmic progression instead

## Scene Job Thinking

Every scene has exactly one job. Name it before designing the visual.

Common scene jobs:
- **hook**: stop the thumb. Visual must work in 0.3 seconds without text.
- **context**: orient the viewer. Who, what, why. Clear layout, moderate energy.
- **proof**: show evidence. The evidence IS the visual, not a decoration beside text.
- **build**: advance the argument. Each build scene adds one idea.
- **peak**: deliver maximum impact. This is where scale drama lives.
- **rest**: let the viewer absorb. Negative space, slow motion, fewer elements.
- **payoff**: land the conclusion. Return to or surpass the hook's energy.
- **close**: clean exit. CTA if needed, but earned, not forced.

How to use this:
- write the job name on every scene card in the story mother
- two consecutive scenes should not have the same job (two "build" scenes in a row = energy flatline)
- the job constrains the visual: a "rest" scene with 6 animated elements is not resting. A "peak" scene at 80px font is not peaking.

## What Great Scenes Actually Look Like

These are not templates. They are thinking patterns — descriptions of what the viewer experiences when a scene is doing its job well.

### A great hook scene

The viewer sees ONE dominant visual element that creates a question or a tension. There is no title. There is no subtitle. There is no "welcome to my video." There is only a frame that makes the viewer need to know what comes next.

Think:
- a number so large it fills 70% of the frame, with a single line of context below it
- a screenshot of something that should not exist, zoomed to the detail that proves it
- a before/after split where the difference is visible in 0.3 seconds without reading
- a face or product in extreme close-up with one word that reframes it

The energy is 8-10. The motion is fast (150-250ms). The color breaks from whatever the feed around it looks like.

### A great proof scene

The evidence owns the frame. Text supports it, not the other way around.

Think:
- the screenshot fills 70-80% of the frame, inside a device container (browser chrome, phone bezel) that grounds it in reality
- a bright callout arrow or highlight box draws the eye to the exact detail that matters
- the surrounding frame is darker or blurred, creating depth that pushes the evidence forward
- the entrance is a zoom-in from a wider context shot, so the viewer understands where this evidence lives

The energy matches the evidence's importance: hero evidence at energy 8-9, supporting evidence at 5-6.

### A great data/number scene

The number is the largest element on screen. Not 80px. Not 120px. Think 200-300px, filling the horizontal width, with a single line of context at 36-48px below.

Think:
- the number enters with a snap (150ms, slight overshoot), not a gentle spring
- the background is a contrasting color from the previous scene (if the previous scene was dark, this one is bright, or vice versa)
- there is nothing else competing for attention — no bullet lists, no secondary information
- the number's color is the accent color (10% in the 60:30:10 budget), used nowhere else at this scale

### A great rest scene

The screen breathes. One element, or nothing but ambient texture. The viewer's brain processes what it just learned.

Think:
- negative space is 70%+ of the frame
- a single text line or a small icon, centered or off-center, sits quietly
- the background shifts subtly (a slow gradient drift, a gentle particle field)
- motion is slow (600-1000ms) or absent
- this scene is 2-4 seconds. Long enough to feel the pause, short enough to not bore.

Rest scenes make the peaks around them feel stronger. They are not filler — they are contrast.

### A great comparison scene

Two things, side by side or sequential, with a clear visual hierarchy showing which wins.

Think:
- split screen where the "before" side is desaturated/smaller/dimmer and the "after" side is vibrant/larger/brighter
- the split is not 50/50 — the winning side gets 60% of the frame
- a clear visual signal marks the winner (checkmark, glow, scale increase)
- the comparison enters sequentially (losing side first, pause, winning side), not simultaneously
- a single word or short phrase anchors each side

## Color Story Thinking

Color is not decoration — it is narrative. The color journey across scenes tells a story even when the viewer is not consciously reading it.

Think about the video's color in three acts:
- **Act 1**: establish the world. What color family says "this is a [tech/business/creative/science] topic"? Commit to it.
- **Act 2**: vary within the world. Scene-to-scene temperature shifts (warm for human moments, cool for technical moments, bright for evidence, dark for atmosphere) create rhythm without breaking the world.
- **Act 3**: resolve. Return to Act 1's palette, possibly brighter/warmer, signaling conclusion and confidence.

Practical color moves:
- if the previous scene was cool blue, make this scene warm amber. The shift is subtle but the viewer feels "something changed."
- evidence scenes can break the color world briefly (a bright white screenshot on a dark background creates instant focus)
- the hook scene's color should contrast with what Douyin's feed typically looks like in this genre (if every tech video is dark blue, consider starting with a warm or bright hook)

## Evidence Treatment Ladder

Evidence has a hierarchy. Treat each piece according to its role, not with the same default container.

**Tier 1 — Hero evidence** (the proof that makes or breaks the thesis):
- near-full-frame (70-90% of screen)
- device container that grounds it (browser, phone, terminal)
- animated entrance (zoom from overview, or blur-to-sharp reveal)
- highlighted region with callout
- stays on screen long enough to verify (3-5 seconds)
- the scene's other elements are minimal — the evidence IS the scene

**Tier 2 — Supporting evidence** (reinforces a point already made):
- contained in a card or device frame at 40-60% of screen
- positioned beside text that explains what to notice
- one highlighted region
- 2-3 seconds on screen

**Tier 3 — Background evidence** (establishes credibility without demanding attention):
- reduced opacity (0.3-0.5) behind text
- blurred or partially cropped
- no highlight or callout
- the viewer knows it exists but does not need to read it

## Scene-Level Decision Card

Before visual direction, every scene should have these decisions explicitly made:

1. **Job**: what is this scene's one job? (hook / context / proof / build / peak / rest / payoff / close)
2. **Energy level**: 1-10, assigned during story mother, constrains visual intensity
3. **Layout pattern**: which layout serves this job? (full-frame / asymmetric split / stacked reveal / negative space / grid)
4. **Color temperature**: warm / cool / neutral / bright? How does it contrast with the previous scene?
5. **Scale moment**: is this a scale drama scene? If yes, what element gets extreme scale?
6. **Evidence role**: if evidence is present, is it hero / supporting / background?
7. **Motion character**: snappy (150-250ms) / smooth (300-500ms) / slow (600-1000ms) / held?
8. **Density**: minimal (1-2 elements) / moderate (3-5) / dense (6+)?
9. **Estimated duration** (`est_duration_sec`): how long should this scene be?
10. **Creator signature role**: absent / identity marker / host cue / transition mark / emotional return. If present, explain why it helps this scene and what proof/subtitle region it must avoid.

Duration thinking:
- rest scenes: 2-4 seconds (long enough to breathe, short enough not to bore)
- hook scenes: 0.5-3 seconds (the viewer decided to stay or leave)
- proof scenes: 3-5 seconds per information unit (the viewer needs time to verify)
- build scenes: 5-15 seconds (one idea, clearly developed)
- peak/payoff scenes: 3-8 seconds (maximum impact, then move)
- if a scene exceeds 30 seconds, ask whether it should be split into two scenes with different jobs
- if total video duration / scene count > 25 seconds average, review whether scenes carry unnecessary padding

These decisions flow from the scene's job and energy. In Lean mode, keep only film-changing `job`, three-level `energy`, speech cues, contrast target, and candidate hypotheses under `extensions.visual_intent.scenes[]`.

## The "Best Creator" Self-Test

Before finalizing visual direction, run this self-test:

1. If a top Douyin creator in this topic area saw this video in their feed, would they stop scrolling? If not, the hook needs work.
2. If the film declares a hero scene, is its decisive frame unmistakable? If no hero is declared, does the sequence still create memorable contrast or progression?
3. If you watch the video at 2x speed with no audio, can you still feel when the important moments happen? If everything looks the same speed, the energy curve is flat.
4. Would you screenshot any frame to send to a friend? If not, the video is informative but not memorable.
5. If you showed 4 consecutive middle scenes as stills to someone who has never seen the video, could they guess the scene order? If the scenes are interchangeable, the visual direction lacks progression.

These are not pass/fail gates — they are thinking prompts that force honest evaluation before execution begins.

## Integration With Stage Machine

**During story mother construction:**
- assign energy levels to every scene
- identify an optional hero scene or choose a sequence-led strategy
- name each scene's job
- sketch the three-act energy shape

**During visual direction:**
- run the source material director pass first, so every scene's picture design comes from current material and recording rhythm
- fill out the scene decision card for every scene
- design the hero frame first only when one is intentionally selected
- design the hook second
- design remaining scenes to create contrast with the hero and hook
- run the "best creator" self-test

**During lookdev:**
- verify that rendered scenes match their assigned energy levels
- verify the selected hero frame is dominant, or verify the sequence-led strategy has readable progression
- verify that scene-to-scene contrast is visible at 2x speed
- verify that the energy curve reads clearly

## State Writeback

In Lean mode, write only rendered-film decisions to `extensions.visual_intent`:

- optional `hero_scene_id`
- per-scene `job`, `energy`, `speech_cues`, `contrast_from_previous`, and critical `candidate_hypotheses`

Extended projects may retain their historical `visual.*` decision maps.
